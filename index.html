Overview

SYLARAS (SYstemic Lymphoid Architecture Response ASsessment) is a preclinical research platform for the interrogation of systemic immune response to disease and therapy. The approach combines multiplex immunophenotyping with biological computation to transform complex single-cell datasets into a visual compendium of the time and tissue-dependent changes occurring in immune cell frequency and/or function in response to an arbitrary immune stimulus (e.g. tumor model, infectious or autoimmune disease, vaccine, immunotherapy, etc.).

SYLARAS is deployed in three stages. In the first stage, longitudinal immunophenotyping data are collected from mouse lymphoid organs of test and control subjects in a high-throughput manner by multiplex flow cytometry. In the second stage, raw FCS files are spectrally compensated and filtered for viable cells before undergoing a systematic immune cell subset identification procedure. In the final stage, the pre-processed data are computationally analyzed using an open-source data analysis tool scripted in the Python programming language and run at the command-line of a personal computer. This leads to the generation of a set of data-rich graphical dashboards (1 per immune cell type) that together portray systemic immune response to any given experimental perturbation.

Motivation

The ability to devise safe and effective medicines that elicit a targeted immune response to cancer or infectious disease hinges on a more integrated understanding of the cell and molecular mechanisms governing systemic immunology. Such a holistic perspective can only be achieved through detailed studies of immune structure and function using preclinical model systems that permit examination of multiple lymphoid organs and which preserve the context of complex immunologic repertoires in high fidelity.

Although state of the art technologies for single-cell data acquisition allow for the rapid collection of expansive datasets pertinent to the study of systemic immune response, a companion data analysis tool capable of fully automating the rigorous statistical analysis and visual display is lacking. By leveraging a set of powerful Python-based computational libraries against bulky single-cell datasets spanning time, tissue, experimental perturbation, and biological replicate, SYLARAS overcomes the labor-intensity of manual cell subset identification, comprehensive statistical analysis, and graphical representation of single-cell data at scale.


Principle

Immune activation (or suppression) induces proliferation, migration, and differentiation of the biologically-specialized immune cells comprising a lymphoid tissue's cellular architecture. Although the molecular mechanisms underlying these changes may not always be clear, the resultant redistribution in immune cell frequency is readily quantified by immunophenotyping: a technique for delineating the proportions of cell types based on differential antigen expression. SYLARAS capitalizes on this concept to infer specific immune cell lineages and their network-level architectures perturbed by a given immune stimulus. The ability of SYLARAS to create ample content for inductive scientific inference makes it a broadly useful preclinical research tool.


Cell Subset Identification

When immunoreactivity is reported in binary terms (e.g. CDa+, CDb-, CDc+), the number of possible immunophenotypes specified by a panel of M antibodies is 2M. This exponential relationship makes comprehensive cell subset identification through serial gating strategy cumbersome, time consuming, and non-commutative. SYLARAS avoids these drawbacks of cell subset identification through point-and-click analysis programs by specifying a cell immunoreactivity on a channel-by-channel basis using a computer-assisted gating procedure.

In this procedure, the interface between background and foreground signal is defined for each immunomarker by curating a set of unidimensional gates based on signal intensity distributions (i.e. histograms). To aid in objective gate placement, the signal intensity distribution of unlabeled/isotype labeled cells is super-imposed on the immunolabeled distribution to reveal how autofluorescence/off-target antibody binding compares with experimental data ( panel a ).

Histograms are then programmatically transformed to center the gate at zero, causing background signal intensities to become negative valued. Each cell in the dataset can then be assigned an M-dimensional Boolean vector fully specifying its immunophenotype by binarizing signal intensity values according to their sign (+/-) ( panel b ). For example, a cell assigned the Boolean vector [1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1 ,0, 0] might correspond to the immunophenotype: CDa+, CDb-+, CDc+, CDd+, CDe-, CDf-, CDg-, CDh+, CDi+, CDj-, CDk-

In the case of an immunomarker panel of 3 antibodies, the vectorization procedure can be geometrically conceptualized as the binning of cells across the 23 (= 8) octants of the 3-dimensional cube ( panel c ). Extrapolating this principle to higher dimensions (i.e. more immunomarkers), the vectorization procedure is equivalent to the binning of cells across the 2M orthants of an M-dimensional hypercube.


Workflow (experimental)

(1) An immune stimulus (glioblastoma brain cancer shown here) or control agent is administered to respective cohorts of age-matched mice. (2) Lymphoid tissues are harvested from mice of each treatment group at various time points after administration of the immune stimulus. (3) Tissues are processed into single-cell suspensions and plated in a 96 well V-bottom plate. (4) Cells are immunolabeled with a cocktail of fluorophore-conjugated antibodies then stained with a fixable viability dye (FVD). (5) Data are acquired by 12-color high-throughput flow cytometry. (6) Raw data are spectrally deconvolved and selected for viable singlets. (7) Pre-processed data undergo a computer-assisted gating procedure (see the "Cell Subset Identification" tab above for details) prior to computational data analysis. SYLARAS portrays longitudinal, multi-organ immunophenotyping data on a per cell type basis in a readily-interpretable dashboard interface.
